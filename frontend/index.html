Indonesia as a Global Biodiversity Hotspot

Indonesia is recognised as one of the world’s most biodiverse nations. The country contains more than 17,000 islands and supports thousands of species of plants, mammals, birds, and marine life.

Tropical rainforests across Indonesia provide critical habitat for many endangered species including the Javan Rhinoceros, Sumatran Tiger, and Javan Eagle. Protecting these ecosystems is essential for maintaining ecological balance and preventing species extinction.

Digital platforms like Komodo Hub help increase awareness while encouraging students and communities to contribute to conservation efforts through observation reporting and environmental education.

Threats to Biodiversity

Major threats to wildlife in Indonesia include deforestation, illegal wildlife trade, climate change, and habitat fragmentation. Rapid agricultural expansion and urban development can destroy natural habitats if not managed sustainably.

Environmental Education

Environmental education helps students understand how ecosystems function and why biodiversity protection is important. When young people learn about conservation early, they are more likely to support sustainable environmental practices.

Community Conservation

Community-based conservation programs encourage local people to participate in protecting natural habitats. Communities living near forests and national parks often play a vital role in monitoring wildlife and reporting environmental changes.
